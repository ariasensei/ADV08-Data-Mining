Part 1: Foundations

What is Clustering? 🔍

Automatically grouping similar items together without being told what the groups are.

The Core Idea

Clustering is the process of grouping similar data points together based on their characteristics, without being told in advance what the groups should be.

Think of it like sorting a pile of unlabeled photos: without being told "these are dogs, these are cats," you naturally group photos that look similar together.

🔑 Clustering vs Classification: The Key Difference

✅ Classification (Supervised)

  • • You have labels ready (Spam/Not Spam)
  • • Model learns from labeled examples
  • • Predicts category for new data
  • • Example: "Is this email spam?"

❓ Clustering (Unsupervised)

  • • No labels exist yet
  • • Model finds natural groups itself
  • • Discovers patterns in unlabeled data
  • • Example: "What groups exist in this data?"

Part 2: Core Tools

Distance Measures 📏

How do computers decide which items are "similar"? They use distance metrics.

Why Distance Matters

Clustering groups points that are close together and separates points that are far apart. But how do we measure "close" or "far"?

That's where distance measures come in. They calculate the similarity between two data points as a number.

Key Idea: Smaller distance = more similar. Larger distance = less similar.

📐 Euclidean Distance (Most Common)

The straight-line distance between two points, like measuring with a ruler on a map.

distance = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]

In English: Find the difference in each direction, square them, add them up, and take the square root.

Example: Two students

Student A: 70 hours studied, 85 test score

Student B: 75 hours studied, 90 test score

Distance = √[(75-70)² + (90-85)²] = √[25 + 25] = √50 = 7.07

Lower number = more similar study patterns

✓ Best for:

Continuous numerical data (age, height, test scores, prices)

🏙️ Manhattan Distance (City Blocks)

Distance like walking around city blocks instead of cutting diagonally. Also called "taxicab distance."

distance = |x₂-x₁| + |y₂-y₁|

In English: Find the difference in each direction (absolute value), then add them together.

Example: Same two students

Student A: 70 hours studied, 85 test score

Student B: 75 hours studied, 90 test score

Distance = |75-70| + |90-85| = 5 + 5 = 10

Usually larger than Euclidean (doesn't cut diagonals)

✓ Best for:

When you can only move in straight lines (city grids, inventory paths)

📄 Cosine Similarity (Text & Angles)

Measures the angle between two vectors. Perfect for comparing text documents or preferences.

similarity = (A · B) / (|A| × |B|)
distance = 1 - similarity

In English: Measures how aligned two directions are (0° = same direction, 90° = perpendicular)

Example: Movie preferences

Person A: Likes [Action:9, Drama:3, Comedy:2]

Person B: Likes [Action:8, Drama:4, Comedy:1]

High cosine similarity = similar taste in movies

Not about actual values, but about direction/pattern

✓ Best for:

Text documents, user preferences, recommendation systems

🔤 Hamming Distance (Categories)

Counts how many positions are different between two strings or categories.

distance = number of different positions

Example: DNA sequences

DNA A: AGGTAB

DNA B: AGGTAC

Distance = 1 (only the last letter is different)

✓ Best for:

Categorical data, strings, DNA sequences, binary attributes

🎯 Which Distance to Choose?

Customer Demographics (age, income, location):

Use Euclidean Distance

Movie/Music Recommendations:

Use Cosine Similarity

Gene Sequences or DNA:

Use Hamming Distance

GPS Routing or Urban Planning:

Use Manhattan Distance

Part 4: Methods

Clustering Algorithms ⚙️

Different ways to find groups in data.

🎯 K-Means: Centroid-Based

Divides data into k clusters by finding the center of each group and assigning points to the nearest center.

How it works (Simple):

  1. 1. Pick k random starting points (cluster centers)
  2. 2. Assign each data point to the nearest center
  3. 3. Move centers to the middle of their assigned points
  4. 4. Repeat steps 2-3 until clusters stop changing

✓ Pros:

Fast, simple, works well with medium datasets

✗ Cons:

Need to specify k, finds spherical clusters only

🌳 Hierarchical Clustering: Tree Structure

Creates a tree (dendrogram) showing how data points and clusters group together at different levels.

How it works:

Bottom-up approach: Start with each point as its own cluster, then merge closest clusters

Result: Tree showing relationships at all distances

✓ Pros:

See structure at all levels, don't need to specify k

✗ Cons:

Slower, harder to interpret for large datasets

🌀 DBSCAN: Density-Based

Groups together points that are close in dense regions. Great for finding irregular shapes.

Key idea:

A cluster is a group of nearby points. Points in empty space are considered noise (outliers).

✓ Pros:

Finds any shape, detects outliers automatically

✗ Cons:

Hard to tune parameters, slower

Part 5: Process

Clustering Workflow 🔄

Step-by-step process from raw data to meaningful groups.

1

🗂️ Collect Data

Gather data about items you want to group (customer behavior, product features, gene expressions, etc.)

2

🧹 Clean & Normalize

Remove missing values, handle outliers, scale features to similar ranges (so height doesn't dominate income)

3

📏 Choose Distance Measure

Pick the right metric (Euclidean for numbers, Cosine for text, Hamming for categories)

4

⚙️ Select Algorithm

Choose K-Means (fast, simple), Hierarchical (see all levels), or DBSCAN (any shape)

5

🎯 Tune Parameters

For K-Means: decide on k (number of clusters). Use elbow method or silhouette scores

6

🚀 Run Clustering

Execute the algorithm. Get cluster assignments for each data point

7

📊 Visualize & Interpret

Plot clusters, examine characteristics of each group, give them meaningful names

Part 6: Deep Dive

Real-World Scenario 🌍

Complete example: A coffee company wants to understand its customers.

☕ Case Study: Starbucks Customer Segmentation

The Problem: Starbucks has 30 million customers worldwide. The company wants to:

  • • Send better personalized offers
  • • Improve in-store experience for different customer types
  • • Reduce waste by stocking what each segment likes

Step 1️⃣: Data Collection

What data do they collect?

  • • Purchase frequency (daily, weekly, monthly?)
  • • Average spend per visit ($)
  • • Favorite drink type (espresso, latte, frappuccino?)
  • • Time of day (morning rush, afternoon break, evening?)
  • • Add-ons bought (pastries, sandwiches, merch?)
  • • Loyalty program status (gold member, new?)

Step 2️⃣: Clean & Normalize

Sample raw data:

Customer A: visits=52/year, spend=$3.50/visit, favorite=latte, time=morning
Customer B: visits=200/year, spend=$5.00/visit, favorite=frappuccino, time=afternoon
Customer C: visits=10/year, spend=$12/visit, favorite=cold-brew, time=evening

They normalize: visits (0-365), spend (0-20), time (0-24 hours)

Step 3️⃣: Choose Distance (Euclidean)

Why Euclidean? All features are numerical (frequency, price, time)

Calculate distance between customers based on their profile

Step 4️⃣: Pick Algorithm (K-Means)

Use K-Means because it's fast for millions of customers and finds clear groups

Step 5️⃣: Decide on k

Testing different k values:

  • • k=2: Too broad, misses important differences
  • • k=4: Good! Makes sense and shows clear patterns ✓
  • • k=8: Too many, hard to manage and interpret

Decision: Use k=4 clusters

🎯 Step 6️⃣: Results - The 4 Customer Segments

💼 Cluster 1: "Business Professionals"

Profile: Visit daily, spend $4/visit, always espresso or americano, rush hour, high loyalty

Size: 8M customers | Revenue: High ($600M/year)

Strategy: Morning rush express line, loyalty rewards, "double shot" deals

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Cluster 2: "Social Visitors"

Profile: Visit 2x/week, spend $6/visit, love lattes & pastries, afternoon/weekend, medium loyalty

Size: 12M customers | Revenue: Very high ($720M/year)

Strategy: Comfy seating, wifi, food promotions, group discounts

🎓 Cluster 3: "Students"

Profile: Visit 3x/week, spend $2.50/visit, frappuccinos & pastries, long stays, low loyalty

Size: 7M customers | Revenue: Medium ($260M/year)

Strategy: Student discounts, study-hour promo, happy hour drinks

💎 Cluster 4: "Premium Enthusiasts"

Profile: Visit rarely (1x/month), spend $12/visit, specialty drinks, any time, very high loyalty

Size: 3M customers | Revenue: High ($432M/year)

Strategy: VIP lounge, exclusive flavors, personalized recommendations

💰 Business Results

  • ✓ Targeted campaigns: 40% increase in offer acceptance
  • ✓ Inventory optimization: Reduced waste by 20%
  • ✓ Revenue growth: $2B additional annual revenue from better targeting
  • ✓ Customer satisfaction: Higher NPS (net promoter score) across all segments
